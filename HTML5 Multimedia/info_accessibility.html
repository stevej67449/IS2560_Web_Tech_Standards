Accessibility

Overview

The goal of multimedia accessibility is to make audio and video content available to users who may have sensory impairments, language difficulties, require devices like a screen reader, or who can’t otherwise view/hear the content due to other limitations. If you would like an overview of the different types of limitations a user may have and how to provide accommodations for them, check out the table below.

Common Accessibility Issues [1]

Subtitles

To make video elements accessible for people with hearing difficulties or users unable to play sound subtitles are a widely accepted practice. The two most popular subtitling formats currently in use are SRT (SubRip Subtitle) and WebVTT (Web Video Text Tracks). These files sync audio cues to timecodes an allow text do display in time to visuals.

If you have a caption/subtitle file prepared it is really very easy to add it to a video. Within the video tag you will add a new <track> tag much like you would add a <source> tag. This track tag will tell the video player what kind of text is being used, where it is stored, and what language the text is for. Sites with international users can include multiple language subtitle tracks and allow users to select their language of choice.

Here’s what the HTML for adding a subtitle would look like:


<video controls='controls'>
    <source src="video/vid1.mp4" type="video/mp4">
    <track src="video/subtitles.srt" kind="subtitles" srclang="en">
   Your browser doesn't support HTML5 Video.
</video>

Multimedia Controls

The appearance of a default multimedia element will differ depending on the browser accessing the website. Each of the major vendors has minor cosmetic tweak towards presenting a video and control scheme. Fortunately, despite looking different browser to browser, the controls all feature mostly the same functionality and behave similarly.

Currently only some browsers have keyboard enabled media controls. Developers should be aware of this and attempt to add custom audio and video controls to their pages. Luckily, some simple JavaScript is all it takes to make multimedia content behave consistently. If you are interested in adding better controls for your multimedia, be sure to check out this multimedia accessibility guide from the Mozilla Developers network. Power users may also be interested in customizing the appearance of the default player by adding unique CSS and JavaScript to their code.
