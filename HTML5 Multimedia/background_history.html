Pre HTML5

In The Beginning

In the early days of the modern “consumer” internet experience, text was the main method of communication. Images took a long time to load on the page, video content could easily be interrupted by other traffic mid-download leading to broken web page elements.
For many, udio and video were only available in the form of digitized MIDI tunes and animated GIFs.[1]

Internet users always had a demand for high quality multimedia content, but they had to wait for technology to catch up their needs. As access to higher bandwidth connections became more available/affordable, the more people finally had access to online video and audio content that they had been craving. Unfortunately, the even after the technology advanced there were still more growing pains before accessing multimedia content on a website became an effortless experience.

Plug-in Pandemonium

Past iterations of HTML tended to rely on third-party plug-ins to achieve video playback. Many industry giants stepped up hoping to fill a need and thus plugins like Adobe Flash, Apple QuickTime, and Microsoft Windows Media Player all had periods of popularity.[3]

This fractured technology ecosystem had some major drawbacks. Cross-browser compatibility was a challenge because vendors would generally only support technologies that they had invested in or developed. This meant that web surfers might miss out on multimedia content if it was developed using a non-supported media extension.
